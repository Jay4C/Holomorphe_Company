Quality management based on the annex 1 of the Directive 2006/42/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 17 May 2006 on machines and amending Directive 95/16/EC
Essential health and safety requirements for the design and construction of machines Solutions
GENERAL PRINCIPLES
1. The manufacturer of a machine or his agent must ensure that a risk assessment is made to determine the health and safety requirements that apply to the machine. The machine must then be designed and constructed taking into account the results of the risk assessment. Through the iterative process for assessing and reducing the risks referred to above, the manufacturer or his agent:
- determines the limits of the machine, including its normal use and any reasonably predictable use,
- lists the dangers that may arise from the machine and the associated dangerous situations,
- considers the risks, given the severity of a possible injury or undermining the health and probability,
- evaluates the risks, with a view to determining whether a risk reduction is necessary, in accordance with the purpose of this Directive,
- eliminates the hazards or reduces the risks associated with these hazards by applying protection measures, according to the priority order set out in point 1.1.2 (b).
2. The obligations provided by the essential health and safety requirements apply only when the corresponding danger exists for the machine considered when used under the conditions laid down by the manufacturer or his agent, but also in abnormal situations predictable. In any case, the principles of integration of the security covered Section 1.1.2 and the obligations concerning the marking of the machines and the instruction instructions covered section 1.7.3 and 1.7.4 apply.
3. The essential health and safety requirements set out in this Annex are mandatory. However, given the state of the art, the objectives they fix may not be achieved. In this case, the machine must, as far as possible, be designed and constructed to extend to these objectives.
4. This Annex includes several parties. The first has a general scope and is applicable to all types of machines. Other parties target certain types of more particular hazards. Nevertheless, it is imperative to consider the full part of this Annex in order to ensure that all relevant essential requirements. When designing a machine, the requirements of the general part and the requirements of one or more of the other parts of the Annex are taken into account, according to the results of the risk assessment carried out in accordance with point 1 present general principles.
1. ESSENTIAL HEALTH REQUIREMENTS AND SAFETY
1.1. Generality
1.1.1. Definition

For the purposes of this Annex, it is meant by:
a) "danger": a potential source of injury or health infringement;
b) "danger zone" means any area inside and / or around a machine in which a person is subject to a risk for his safety or health;
c) "Person exposed" means anyone who is entirely or partially in a dangerous zone;
d) "operator": the person (s) charged (s) to install, operate, adjust, maintain, clean, troubleshoot or move a machine;
e) "Risk" means the combination of the probability and severity of a health injury or damage that may occur in a hazardous situation;
f) "protector": machine element used specifically to provide protection by means of a hardware barrier;
g) "protection device": device (other than a protector) that reduces the risk, alone or associated with a protector;
h) "normal use": use of a machine according to the information provided in the instruction manual;
i) "reasonably foreseeable misuse": use of the machine in a manner not provided for in the instructions, but which is likely to result from easily predictable human behavior.
1.1.2. Principles of Security Integration
a) The machine must be designed and constructed to be able to ensure its function and so that it can be operated, adjustable and maintain without exposing anyone at a risk when these operations are made under the conditions provided by the manufacturer. but also taking into account any reasonably predictable misuse.
The actions taken must have the objective of deleting any risk during the predictable duration of the machine, including the transport, assembly, disassembly, decommissioning and disposal phases.
b) In choosing the most suitable solutions, the manufacturer or his authorized representative must apply the following principles, in the order indicated:
- eliminate or reduce risks as far as possible (integration of safety into the design and construction of the machine),
- take the necessary protective measures against risks that cannot be eliminated,
- inform users of the residual risks due to the incomplete effectiveness of the protective measures adopted, indicate whether special training is required and indicate whether it is necessary to provide personal protective equipment.
c) When designing and constructing the machinery and when drawing up the instructions, the manufacturer or his authorized representative must consider not only normal use of the machinery but also any reasonably foreseeable misuse.
The machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to prevent it from being used in an abnormal manner, if such use creates a risk. Where applicable, the instructions must draw the user's attention to the contraindications to the use of the machine which, according to experience, could arise.
d) The machinery must be designed and constructed to take account of the constraints imposed on the operator by the necessary or foreseeable use of personal protective equipment.
e) The machine must be delivered with all essential special equipment and accessories so that it can be adjusted, maintained and used in complete safety.
1.1.3. Materials and products
The materials used for the construction of the machine or the products employed or created during its use must not endanger the health and safety of persons. In particular, when using fluids, the machinery must be designed and built to avoid risks due to filling, use, recovery and disposal.
1.1.4. Lighting
The machine must be provided with built-in lighting, suitable for operations where, despite ambient lighting having normal intensity, the absence of such a device could create a risk.
The machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that there are no disturbing shadows, irritating glare or dangerous stroboscopic effects on moving parts due to lighting.
The interior parts which must be inspected and adjusted frequently, as well as maintenance areas, must be equipped with suitable lighting devices.
1.1.5. Machine design for handling
The machine, or each of its parts, must:
— be capable of being handled and transported in complete safety,
— be packaged or designed so that they can be stored safely and without damage.
During the transport of the machine and/or its components, it must not be possible to produce unexpected movements or dangers due to instability, if the machine and/or its components are handled according to the instructions.
When the mass, dimensions or shape of the machinery or its parts do not allow it to be moved by hand, the machinery or each of its parts must:
— either be equipped with accessories allowing gripping by a means of lifting,
— either be designed in such a way that it can be equipped with such accessories,
— or be of such a shape that normal lifting means can be easily accommodated.
When the machine or one of its elements has to be moved manually, it must:
— either be easily movable,
— or include gripping means allowing it to be moved in complete safety.
Special provisions must be made for the handling of tools and/or machine parts which, even light ones, can be dangerous.
1.1.6. Ergonomics
Under the intended conditions of use, discomfort, fatigue and physical and psychological stress on the operator must be reduced to a minimum taking into account the following ergonomic principles:
— take into account the variability of the operators with regard to their morphological data, their strength and their resistance,
— provide enough space for the movements of the different parts of the operator's body,
— avoid a work rhythm determined by the machine,
— avoid monitoring that requires prolonged concentration,
— adapt the man-machine interface to the foreseeable characteristics of the operators.
1.1.7. Workplace
The workstation must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid any risk due to exhaust gases and/or lack of oxygen.
If the machinery is intended to be used in a hazardous environment, presenting risks to the health and safety of the operator, or if the machinery itself is the source of a hazardous environment, means must be provided sufficient to provide the operator with good working conditions and protection against any foreseeable danger.
Where applicable, the workstation must be equipped with an adequate cabin designed, constructed and/or equipped to meet the above conditions. The exit must allow rapid evacuation. In addition, an emergency exit in a direction different from the normal exit should be provided, if necessary.
1.1.8. Seat
Where appropriate and where working conditions permit, workstations which are an integral part of the machinery must be designed for the installation of seats.
If the operator is expected to be in a seated position during his work and if the workstation is an integral part of the machine, the seat must be supplied with the machine.
The operator's seat must provide him with a stable position. In addition, the seat and the distance separating it from the service devices must be able to be adapted to the operator.
If the machinery is subjected to vibrations, the seat must be designed and constructed in such a way as to reduce the vibrations transmitted to the operator to the lowest level reasonably possible. The seat anchorage must withstand all the stresses it may experience. If there is no floor under the operator's feet, the latter must have non-slip footrests.
1.2. CONTROL SYSTEMS
1.2.1. Safety and reliability of control systems
Control systems must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid any dangerous situation. Above all, they must be designed and constructed in such a way:
— to be able to withstand service stresses and normal external influences,
— failure of the hardware or software of the control system does not result in a hazardous situation,
— that errors in the logic of the control system do not lead to a dangerous situation,
— reasonably foreseeable human error during operation does not result in a hazardous situation.
Particular attention should be paid to the following points:
— the machine must not start unexpectedly,
— the parameters of the machine must not be changed without an order having been given to that effect, when this change could lead to dangerous situations,
— the machine must not be prevented from stopping if the stop command has already been given,
— no moving part of the machinery or any part held by the machinery must fall or be ejected,
— automatic or manual stopping of moving parts, whatever they may be, must not be prevented,
— the protective devices must remain fully operational or give a stop command,
— the safety-related parts of the control system shall apply consistently to an entire assembly of machinery and/or partly completed machinery.
In the case of wireless control, an automatic shutdown must occur when the correct control signals are not received, in particular in the event of a communication interruption.
1.2.2. Service bodies
Service bodies must be:
— clearly visible and identifiable by means of pictograms, where appropriate,
— placed in such a way that they can be operated in complete safety, without hesitation or loss of time and without ambiguity,
— designed in such a way that the movement of the control devices is consistent with the controlled effect,
— arranged outside the danger zones except, if necessary, for certain service devices, such as an emergency stop and a learning console for the robots,
— located in such a way that activating them cannot create additional risks,
— designed or protected in such a way that the desired effect, if it may entail a hazard, can only be obtained by voluntary action,
— manufactured to withstand foreseeable forces. Particular attention must be paid to emergency stop devices which risk being subjected to strong forces.
When a service device is designed and built to allow several different actions, i.e. its action is not unambiguous, the action commanded must be displayed in clear text and, if necessary, subject to of a confirmation.
The control devices must have a configuration such that their arrangement, their stroke and their resistance are compatible with the controlled action, taking into account the principles of ergonomics.
The machine must be fitted with the signaling devices necessary to operate it safely. From the control station, the operator must be able to read the indications of these devices.
From each control station, the operator must be able to ensure that there is no one in the danger zones, or else the control system must be designed and constructed in such a way that starting is impossible that a person is in the danger zone.
If none of these possibilities is applicable, an audible and/or visual warning signal must be given before the machine is started. Exposed persons must have time to leave the danger zone or prevent the machine from starting.
If necessary, means must be provided so that the machine can only be controlled from control stations located in one or more predetermined zones or locations.
When there are several control stations, the control system must be designed in such a way that the use of one of them prevents the use of the others, except with regard to stop and start devices' emergency stop.
When a machine has several working stations, each station must be provided with all the required control devices without the operators getting in the way or putting each other in a dangerous situation.
1.2.3. Switching on
It must only be possible to start a machine by deliberate action on a control device provided for this purpose.
It is the same:
— for restarting after a shutdown, whatever the cause,
— for controlling a significant change in operating conditions.
However, restarting or modifying the operating conditions can be carried out by voluntary action on a component other than the service component provided for this purpose, provided that this does not lead to a dangerous situation.
In the case of a machine operating in automatic mode, starting up, restarting after a stoppage or modification of the operating conditions can occur without intervention, provided that this does not lead to a dangerous situation.
If a machine has several start-up control devices and the operators can therefore put each other in danger, additional devices must be provided to exclude this risk. If safety requires that starting and/or stopping take place in a determined sequence, devices must be provided to ensure that these operations take place in the exact order.
1.2.4. Stop
1.2.4.1. normal stop
The machine must be fitted with a control device enabling it to be completely stopped in complete safety.
Each workstation must be equipped with a control device allowing all or part of the functions of the machine to be stopped, depending on the existing dangers, so as to make the machine safe.
The machine stop command must take priority over the start commands.
Once the machine or its dangerous functions have been stopped, the energy supply to the actuators concerned must be interrupted.
1.2.4.2. Stopped for service reasons
When, for service reasons, it is necessary to use a stop command which does not interrupt the power supply to the actuators, the stop function must be monitored and maintained.
1.2.4.3. Emergency stop
The machinery must be fitted with one or more emergency stop devices to avoid dangerous situations which are occurring or which are imminent.
The device must:
— include service devices that are clearly identifiable, clearly visible and quickly accessible,
— cause the hazardous process to stop as quickly as possible, without creating additional risk,
— if necessary, trigger or enable the triggering of certain protection movements.
When activating the emergency stop device has ceased after having given a stop command, this command must be maintained by engaging the emergency stop device until it is expressly disabled; it must not be possible to engage the device without operating a stop command; it must only be possible to deactivate the device by appropriate action and it must not have the effect of restarting the machine but only of authorizing a restart.
The emergency stop function must be available and operational at all times, regardless of the operating mode.
Emergency stop devices must support other protective measures and not replace them.
1.2.4.4. Machine sets
In the case of machinery or parts of machinery designed to work together, these must be designed and constructed in such a way that the stop commands, including emergency stop devices, can stop not only the machine, but also all associated equipment if keeping them in operation could constitute a hazard.
1.2.5. Selection of control or operating modes
The selected control or operating mode must have priority over all other control or operating modes, with the exception of emergency stop.
If the machinery has been designed and constructed to allow it to be used in several control or operating modes requiring different protective measures/or working procedures, it must be fitted with a mode selector which can be locked in each position. Each position of the selector must be clearly identifiable and must correspond to a single control or operating mode.
The selector can be replaced by other means of selection making it possible to limit the use of certain functions of the machine to certain categories of operators.
If, for certain operations, the machine must be able to operate when a guard has been moved or removed and/or a protective device has been neutralized, the control or operating mode selector must simultaneously:
— disable all other control or operating modes,
— authorize the implementation of dangerous functions only by control devices requiring maintained action,
— authorize the implementation of dangerous functions only under conditions of reduced risk while avoiding any danger resulting from a sequence of sequences,
— prevent any implementation of dangerous functions by voluntary or involuntary action on the sensors of the machine.
If these four conditions cannot be fulfilled simultaneously, the control or operating mode selector must activate other protective measures designed and constructed in such a way as to guarantee a safe working area.
In addition, from the adjustment station, the operator must have control of the operation of the elements on which he acts.
1.2.6. Power supply failure
The interruption, the re-establishment after an interruption, or the variation, whatever the direction, of the energy supply to the machine must not lead to dangerous situations.
Particular attention should be paid to the following points:
— the machine must not start unexpectedly,
— the parameters of the machine must not be changed without an order having been given to that effect, when this change could lead to dangerous situations,
— the machine must not be prevented from stopping if the stop command has already been given,
— no moving part of the machinery or any part held by the machinery must fall or be ejected,
— automatic or manual stopping of moving parts, whatever they may be, must not be prevented,
— the protective devices must remain fully operational or give a stop command.
1.3. PROTECTIVE MEASURES AGAINST MECHANICAL RISKS
1.3.1. Risk of loss of stability
The machine, as well as its elements and equipment, must be sufficiently stable to prevent overturning, falling or uncontrolled movements during transport, assembly, dismantling and any other action involving the machine.
If the very shape of the machinery or its planned installation does not ensure sufficient stability, appropriate means of attachment must be provided and indicated in the instructions.
1.3.2. Risk of rupture in service
The various parts of the machine as well as the connections between them must be able to withstand the stresses to which they are subjected during use.
The materials used must have sufficient resistance, adapted to the characteristics of the working environment provided by the manufacturer or his representative, in particular with regard to the phenomena of fatigue, aging, corrosion and abrasion.
The instructions must indicate the types and frequencies of inspections and maintenance required for safety reasons. It must indicate, where applicable, the parts subject to wear as well as the replacement criteria.
If, despite the precautions taken, a risk of rupture or bursting remains, the parts concerned must be mounted, arranged and/or protected in such a way that their fragments are retained, thus avoiding dangerous situations.
Rigid or flexible pipes conveying fluids, in particular under high pressure, must be able to withstand the expected internal and external stresses; they must be firmly attached and/or protected so that, in the event of breakage, they cannot cause any risks.
In the case of automatic transport of the material to be machined to the tool, the conditions indicated below must be fulfilled to avoid risks for people:
— during tool/part contact, the tool must have reached its normal working condition,
— when starting and/or stopping the tool (voluntarily or involuntarily), the routing movement and the movement of the tool must be coordinated.
1.3.3. Risks due to falls, ejection of objects
Precautions must be taken to avoid risks due to falling or ejected objects.
1.3.4. Hazards due to surfaces, edges or angles
The accessible parts of the machinery must, insofar as their function allows, have no sharp edges, sharp corners or rough surfaces liable to cause injury.
1.3.5. Risks due to combined machines
When the machine is intended to carry out several different operations with manual recovery of the part between each operation (combined machine), it must be designed and constructed in such a way that each element can be used separately without the other elements presenting a risk for exposed people.
To this end, each of the elements, if not protected, must be able to be started or stopped individually.
1.3.6. Risks due to variations in operating conditions
In the case of operations under different conditions of use, the machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that the choice and adjustment of these conditions can be carried out in a safe and reliable manner.
1.3.7. Moving Parts Hazards
The moving parts of the machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid the risk of contact which could lead to accidents or, where risks persist, be fitted with guards or protective devices.
All the necessary measures must be taken to prevent the involuntary blocking of the moving parts contributing to the work. In cases where, despite the precautions taken, a blockage is likely to occur, the necessary specific protection devices and tools must, where applicable, be provided to enable safe unblocking.
The instructions and, if possible, an indication on the machine must mention these specific protective devices and the way to use them.
1.3.8. Choice of protection against risks caused by moving parts
The guards or protective devices designed to protect against the risks generated by moving parts must be chosen according to the type of risk. The following criteria should be used to facilitate the choice.
1.3.8.1. Moving transmission elements
Guards designed to protect people against the dangers associated with moving transmission parts must be:
— either fixed guards referred to in section 1.4.2.1,
— or movable guards with locking device referred to in section 1.4.2.2.
The latter solution should be adopted if frequent interventions are planned.
1.3.8.2. Mobile elements contributing to the work
Guards or protective devices designed to protect persons against the dangers associated with moving parts contributing to the work must be:
— either fixed guards referred to in section 1.4.2.1,
— either movable guards with locking device referred to in section 1.4.2.2,
— either protective devices referred to in section 1.4.3,
— or a combination of the above.
However, when certain moving parts contributing directly to the work cannot be made completely inaccessible during their operation due to operations which require the intervention of the operator, these parts must be equipped with:
— fixed guards or movable guards with a locking device preventing access to parts of the moving parts not used for the work, and
— adjustable guards referred to in point 1.4.2.3 limiting access to the parts of the moving parts which require access.
1.3.9. Risks due to uncontrolled movements
When a part of a machine has been stopped, any drift from its stop position, whatever the cause except the action on the control devices, must be prevented or must be such that it does not presents no danger.
1.4. CHARACTERISTICS REQUIRED FOR GUARDS AND PROTECTION DEVICES
1.4.1. General requirements
Protectors and protective devices:
— must be of robust construction,
— must be securely held in place,
— must not cause additional hazards,
— must not be easily circumvented or rendered inoperative,
— must be located at a sufficient distance from the danger zone,
— must restrict the view of the work cycle as little as possible, and
— must allow essential interventions for fitting and/or replacing tools as well as for maintenance, limiting access exclusively to the sector where the work is to be carried out, and, if possible, without dismantling the guard or neutralization of the protective device.
In addition, whenever possible, guards should provide protection against ejected or falling materials and objects and against emissions produced by the machine.
1.4.2. Special requirements for guards
1.4.2.1. Fixed guards
Fixed guards must be fixed by means of systems that can only be opened or removed with tools.
The fastening systems must remain attached to the guards or to the machine during the dismantling of the guards.
As far as possible, guards must not be able to remain in place without their fixings.
1.4.2.2. Movable guards with locking device
Movable guards with interlocking device must:
— as far as possible, remain attached to the machine when they are open,
— be designed and constructed in such a way that their adjustment requires voluntary action.
Movable guards must be associated with an interlocking device:
— preventing the operation of hazardous machine functions until they are closed, and
— giving a stop order as soon as they are no longer closed.
When an operator can reach the danger zone before the risk linked to the dangerous functions of a machine has ceased, in addition to the interlocking device, the movable guards must be associated with an interlocking device:
— preventing the operation of hazardous machine functions until the guards are closed and locked, and
— keeping guards closed and locked until the risk of injury from hazardous machine functions has ceased.
Movable guards with interlocking device must be designed in such a way that the absence or failure of one of their components prevents the starting or causes the stopping of the dangerous functions of the machinery.
1.4.2.3. Adjustable guards limiting access
Adjustable guards limiting access to parts of moving parts strictly necessary for work must:
- can be adjusted manually or automatically depending on the nature of the work to be done,
- can be adjusted easily without the aid of a tool.
1.4.3. Special requirements for protective devices
Protective devices must be designed and incorporated into the control system in such a way that:
— moving parts cannot be set in motion as long as the operator can reach them,
— people cannot reach moving parts while they are moving, and
— the absence or failure of one of their components prevents the starting or causes the moving parts to stop.
The adjustment of protective devices must require voluntary action.
1.5. RISKS DUE TO OTHER HAZARDS
1.5.1. Electric power supply
When machinery is supplied with electrical energy, it must be designed, constructed and equipped in such a way as to prevent, or be able to prevent, all dangers of electrical origin.
The safety objectives provided for in Directive 73/23/EEC apply to machinery. However, the obligations concerning the assessment of conformity and the placing on the market and/or putting into service of machinery with regard to hazards arising from electrical energy are governed exclusively by the provisions of this Directive.
1.5.2. Static electricity
Machinery must be designed and constructed to prevent or limit the appearance of potentially dangerous electrostatic charges and/or be equipped with the means to allow them to flow away.
1.5.3. Power supply other than electric
When machinery is powered by energy other than electricity, it must be designed, constructed and equipped in such a way as to avoid all potential risks associated with these energy sources.
1.5.4. Editing errors
Errors likely to be made during the assembly or reassembly of certain parts, which could be the source of risks, must be made impossible by the design and construction of these parts or, failing that, by indications appearing on the parts themselves and/or on their casings. The same indications must appear on the moving parts and/or on their casing when it is necessary to know the direction of movement to avoid a risk.
If necessary, the instruction manual must give additional information on these risks.
When a faulty connection can be the source of risks, erroneous connections must be made impossible by the design or, failing that, by indications appearing on the elements to be connected and, where applicable, on the means of connection.
1.5.5. Extreme temperatures
Provisions must be made to avoid any risk of injury, by contact or from a distance, with machine elements or materials at high or very low temperature.
The necessary measures must also be taken to avoid the risk of ejection of hot or very cold materials or to provide protection against these risks.
1.5.6. Fire
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid any risk of fire or overheating caused by the machinery itself or by gases, liquids, dust, vapors and other substances produced or used by the machinery.
1.5.7. Explosion
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid any risk of explosion caused by the machinery itself or by gases, liquids, dust, vapors and other substances produced or used by the machinery.
The machine must comply with the provisions of the specific Community directives, with regard to the risks of explosion due to its use in an explosive atmosphere.
1.5.8. Noise
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that the risks resulting from the emission of airborne noise are reduced to the lowest level, taking into account technical progress and the availability of means for reducing noise, in particular at the source.
The noise emission level can be assessed against comparative emission data for similar machines.
1.5.9. Vibration
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that the risks resulting from vibrations produced by the machinery are reduced to the lowest level, taking into account technical progress and the availability of means to reduce vibrations, in particular at the source.
The vibration level can be assessed against comparative emissions data for similar machines.
1.5.10. Radiation
Unwanted machine radiation must be eliminated or reduced to levels that do not adversely affect people.
Any functional ionizing radiation emitted by the machine must be limited to the lowest level necessary for the proper functioning of the machine when installing, operating and cleaning it. When a risk exists, the necessary protective measures must be taken.
Any functional non-ionizing radiation emitted by the machine during its installation, operation and cleaning must be limited to levels which do not have an adverse effect on people.
1.5.11. External radiation
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that external radiation does not interfere with its operation.
1.5.12. Laser radiation
When using laser equipment, the following provisions should be taken into account:
— the laser equipment on a machine must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid unintentional radiation,
— the laser equipment on a machine must be shielded in such a way that neither useful radiation, nor radiation produced by reflection or scattering, nor secondary radiation is harmful to health,
— the optical equipment for observing or adjusting the laser equipment on a machine must be such that no risk to health is created by the laser radiation.
1.5.13. Emission of hazardous materials and substances
Machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to avoid the risk of inhalation, ingestion, contact with the skin, eyes and mucous membranes, and percutaneous penetration of the hazardous materials and substances it produces.
Where the risk cannot be eliminated, machinery must be equipped in such a way that hazardous materials and substances can be contained, evacuated, precipitated by spraying water, filtered or treated by any other equally effective method.
Where the process is not fully contained during normal machine operation, containment and/or escape devices should be placed to have maximum effect.
1.5.14. Risk of being trapped in a machine
The machinery must be designed, constructed or fitted with means to prevent a person from being trapped in it or, if this is not possible, to enable him to call for help.
1.5.15. Risk of slipping, tripping or falling
Parts of the machinery where people are likely to move or stand must be designed and constructed in such a way as to prevent these people from slipping, tripping or falling.
Where applicable, these parts of the machine must be fitted with handrails fixed in relation to the users, enabling them to retain their stability.
1.5.16. Lightning
Machinery requiring protection against the effects of lightning during use must be equipped with a system to discharge the resulting electric charge to earth.
1.6. INTERVIEW
1.6.1. Machine maintenance
Adjustment and maintenance points must be located outside of hazardous areas. Adjustment, maintenance, repair and cleaning operations on the machine and interventions on the machine must be able to be carried out when the machine is stationary.
If one or more of the preceding conditions cannot, for technical reasons, be satisfied, measures must be taken so that these operations can be carried out in complete safety (see section 1.2.5).
In the case of an automated machine and possibly other machines, a connection device for mounting fault diagnosis equipment must be provided.
The parts of an automated machine which are expected to be replaced frequently must be able to be dismantled and reassembled easily and in complete safety. Access to these elements must allow these tasks to be carried out with the necessary technical means according to a planned operating mode.
1.6.2. Access to workstations or intervention points
The machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way as to allow safe access to all places where intervention is necessary during the operation, adjustment and maintenance of the machinery.
1.6.3. Separation of the machine from its energy sources
The machine must be fitted with devices enabling it to be isolated from all sources of energy. These devices must be clearly identified. They must be lockable if the reconnection could present a danger to people. The devices must also be lockable when the operator cannot, from all the places to which he has access, check that the power supply is still cut off.
In the case of a machine which can be supplied with electrical energy by a socket, the removal of the socket is sufficient, provided that the operator can check, from all the places to which he has access, that the socket is still removed. .
After the power supply has been cut off, any residual or stored energy in the circuits of the machine must be able to be evacuated normally, without risk to people.
By way of derogation from the requirement set out in the preceding paragraphs, certain circuits may remain connected to their energy source in order to allow, for example, the maintenance of parts, the backup of information, the lighting of interior parts, etc. In this case, special measures must be taken to ensure the safety of operators.
1.6.4. Operator intervention
The machine must be designed, built and equipped in such a way as to limit operator intervention. If the intervention of an operator cannot be avoided, it must be possible to carry it out easily and in complete safety.
1.6.5. Interior parts cleaning
The machinery must be designed and constructed in such a way that it is possible to clean the internal parts of the machinery which have contained dangerous substances or preparations without entering them; similarly, it must be possible to unblock them from the outside. If it is impossible to avoid entering the machinery, it must be designed and constructed in such a way that cleaning can be carried out in complete safety.
1.7. INFORMATION
1.7.1. Machine Information and Warnings
Information and warnings on the machine should preferably be affixed in the form of easily understood symbols or pictograms. Any written or verbal information and warning must be expressed in one or more official Community language(s) which may be determined in accordance with the Treaty by the Member State in which the machinery is placed on the market and /or putting into service, and accompanied, on request, by versions in any other official Community language understood by the operators.
1.7.1.1. Information and information devices
The information needed to operate a machine must be provided in a form that is unambiguous and easy to understand. This information should not be so excessive as to overburden the operator.
Display screens or any other means of interactive communication between the operator and the machine must be easy to understand and use.
1.7.1.2. Warning devices
Where the health and safety of persons may be endangered by the faulty operation of machinery which operates unsupervised, that machinery must be equipped in such a way as to give an adequate audible or luminous warning.
If the machine is fitted with warning devices, they must not be unambiguous and must be easily perceived. Measures must be taken to enable the operator to check that the warning devices are working at all times.
The provisions of the specific Community directives concerning colors and safety signs must be applied.
1.7.2. Warning on residual risks
Each machine must bear, visibly, legibly and indelibly, the following minimum indications:
— the name and full address of the manufacturer and, where applicable, of his authorized representative,
— the designation of the machine,
— the CE marking (see Annex III),
— designation of the series or type,
— the serial number if it exists,
— the year of construction, ie the year in which the manufacturing process was completed.
It is prohibited to back-date or post-date the machine when affixing the CE marking.
In addition, machinery designed and built for use in an explosive atmosphere must bear this indication.
The machine must also bear all the indications concerning its type, which are essential for its safety of use. This information is subject to the requirements set out in section 1.7.1.
When a part of the machinery must be handled, during its use, with lifting means, its mass must be marked therein in a legible, indelible and unambiguous manner.
1.7.4. Instruction leaflet
Each machine must be accompanied by instructions in the official Community language(s) of the Member State in which the machine is placed on the market and/or put into service.
The instruction manual which accompanies the machine must be an 'original manual' or a 'translation of the original manual', in which case the translation must be accompanied by an 'original manual'.
By way of derogation, the maintenance manual intended for use by specialized personnel who depend on the manufacturer or his authorized representative may be provided in only one of the Community languages understood by this personnel.
The instruction manual must be drafted according to the principles set out below.
1.7.4.1. General principles for drafting the instruction manual
a) The instruction handbook must be drawn up in one or more official languages of the Community. The words "Original instructions" must appear on the language versions of these instructions which have been checked by the manufacturer or his authorized representative.
b) When there is no "Original manual" in the official language(s) of the country in which the machine is used, a translation into this or these language(s) must be provided by the manufacturer or his authorized representative or by the person who enters the machine in the linguistic zone considered. These translations must be marked “Translation of the original notice”.
c) The content of the instruction handbook must not only cover the normal use of the machine, but also take account of reasonably foreseeable misuse.
d) In the case of machinery intended for non-professional users, the drafting and presentation of the instructions must take account of the level of general training and insight which may reasonably be expected of these users.
1.7.4.2. Content of the instruction manual
Each notice must contain, where applicable, at least the following information:
a) the business name and full address of the manufacturer and his authorized representative;
b) the designation of the machine, as indicated on the machine itself, with the exception of the serial number (see section 1.7.3.);
c) the EC declaration of conformity or a document presenting the contents of the EC declaration of conformity, indicating the characteristics of the machine, without necessarily including the serial number and the signature;
d) a general description of the machine;
e) the plans, diagrams, descriptions and explanations necessary for the use, maintenance and repair of the machinery and for checking that it is operating correctly;
f) a description of the workstation(s) likely to be occupied by the operators;
g) a description of the normal use of the machine;
h) warnings concerning contraindications to the use of the machine which, according to experience, may exist;
i) assembly, installation and connection instructions, including plans, diagrams, means of attachment and designation of the frame or installation on which the machinery is to be mounted;
j) installation and mounting instructions to reduce noise and vibration;
k) instructions for commissioning and use of the machinery and, where appropriate, instructions for operator training;
l) information on the residual risks which remain despite the fact that safety has been integrated into the design of the machinery and that additional protective measures and preventive measures have been taken;
m) instructions concerning the protective measures to be taken by users, including, where appropriate, the personal protective equipment to be provided;
n) the essential characteristics of the tools that can be mounted on the machine;
o) the conditions under which the machinery meets the stability requirement during use, transport, assembly or dismantling, when out of service, or during tests or foreseeable breakdowns;
p) instructions for ensuring that transport, handling and storage operations are carried out in complete safety, indicating the mass of the machinery and its various components when they regularly have to be transported separately;
q) the procedure to be followed in the event of an accident or breakdown; if a blockage is likely to occur, the operating procedure to be followed to enable safe unblocking;
r) a description of the adjustment and maintenance operations to be carried out by the user, as well as the preventive measures that must be observed;
s) instructions designed so that adjustment and maintenance can be carried out in complete safety, including the protective measures that must be taken during these operations;
t) specifications for spare parts to be used, where this affects the health and safety of operators;
u) the following airborne noise emission information:
— the A-weighted emission sound pressure level at workstations, when it exceeds 70 dB(A); if this level is less than or equal to 70 dB (A), it should be mentioned,
— the maximum value of the instantaneous C-weighted emission sound pressure at workstations, when it exceeds 63 Pa (130 dB relative to 20 μPa),
— the A-weighted sound power level emitted by the machinery when the A-weighted emission sound pressure level at workstations exceeds 80 dB(A).
These values are either actually measured for the machine in question, or established from measurements made for a technically comparable machine which is representative of the machine to be produced.
Where the machinery is very large, the indication of the A-weighted sound power level may be replaced by the indication of the A-weighted emission sound pressure levels at specified locations around the machinery.
Where harmonized standards are not applied, noise data shall be measured using the method most appropriate for the machine.
When sound emission values are indicated, the uncertainties surrounding these values must be specified.
The operating conditions of the machine during the measurement and the methods used for the measurement shall be described.
When the workstation(s) are not, or cannot be defined, the A-weighted sound pressure level must be measured at 1 m from the surface of the machine and at a height of 1.60 m above the floor or access platform. The position and the value of the maximum sound pressure must be indicated.
Where specific Community directives provide for other requirements for the measurement of sound pressure or power levels, these directives must be applied, and the corresponding requirements of this point do not apply.
v) where the machinery is liable to emit non-ionizing radiation which could harm people, in particular people fitted with active or non-active implantable medical devices, information concerning the radiation emitted for the operator and the people exposed.
1.7.4.3. Commercial documents
The commercial documents presenting the machine must not contradict the instructions with regard to health and safety aspects. Sales literature describing the performance characteristics of the machine must contain the same emissions information as the instruction handbook.
